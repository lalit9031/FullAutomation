The old clock struck midnight.

[pause]

The floorboards groaned beneath unseen weight as Detective Harlow pressed his back against the cold stone wall. He had been following the trail for three weeks—three weeks of dead ends, false names, and one very real body found floating in the Thames.

[sigh]

He checked his revolver. Six rounds. He hoped he would need none of them.

The room beyond the door was dark, but not silent. He could hear breathing. Slow. Patient. As if whatever waited for him on the other side had done this before—had waited for other men in other dark places and had never once needed to hurry.

[dramatic pause]

Harlow pushed open the door.

Standing by the window, silhouetted against the amber glow of London's fog-smothered gas lamps, was a woman in a black dress. She did not turn around. She simply said, in a voice as smooth and cold as river ice:

"I wondered when you would find me, Detective."

He raised his gun.

She finally turned, and the faint light caught the ghost of a smile on her lips. "You of all people should know," she continued, "that the ones who are never suspected... are the ones who planned it that way."

[long pause]

The clock struck one.

And Harlow realised, with a cold certainty that settled like lead in his chest, that he had walked directly into the trap she had laid for him three weeks ago—on the very night it all began.
